Churches

The Bom Jesus do Monte Shrine in Braga, a National Monument and UNESCO World Heritage Site, stands out for its extraordinary surroundings and architectural beauty. It encompasses a Catholic Shrine featuring a church, a monumental stairway symbolizing the Way of the Cross, and a surrounding forest. Notable features include the Basilica, shaped like a Latin cross and one of Portugal's earliest neoclassical buildings, and the impressive stairway divided into three sections: the portico stairway, the stairway of the five senses, and the stairway of the three virtues of faith, hope, and charity. The Santo Ildefonso Church in Porto, rebuilt in the 18th century, showcases exquisite artistry, with its façade adorned with two bell-towers and painted tiles illustrating scenes from the life of Saint Ildefonsus. The Jerónimos Monastery in Lisbon, a pinnacle of Manueline architecture, is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of Portugal's seven wonders. Noteworthy features include its intricate entrances sculpted in honor of King Manuel I. The Batalha Monastery, also known as the Santa Maria da Vitória Monastery, is a Gothic masterpiece built to commemorate a victory over the Castilians. Classified as a UNESCO World Heritage site, it represents an outstanding example of Portuguese Gothic architecture. Learn More

Castles

The National Palace of Pena, perched atop Sintra's highest hill, is renowned for its vibrant facade and strategic location, blending Manueline and Moorish architectural styles. A UNESCO World Heritage site, it evolved from a Middle Ages chapel to a monastery, and finally into a castle. The 16th-century Belém Tower in Lisbon, adorned with symbols of King Manuel I, served as a prison during the Spanish invasion and a defense against pirate attacks, symbolizing Portugal's naval power. The Castle of the Moors, an 8th and 9th-century Moorish fort, offers stunning views from its steep hilltop location but was abandoned in the 15th century. Guimarães Castle, a 10th-century fortress, played a crucial role in Portugal's independence, withstanding the Battle of São Mamede in 1128, earning the title "birthplace of the Portuguese nation." Almourol Castle, situated on a rocky island in the Tagus River, dates back to the 12th century and remains remarkably well-preserved, controlling a strategic waterway and deriving its name from the Arabic word for "high rock."
